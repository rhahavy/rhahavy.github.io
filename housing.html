01Case Study · Real Estate

GTA Housing Market Analysis

A long-run look at prices, inventory, and affordability across the Greater Toronto Area — built to pinpoint when and where the market turned, and who that turn has helped or hurt.

Tableau SQL Excel · 26 years of data · Independent analysis
View the Dashboard View Code on GitHub
01The Question

Where in the GTA has affordability slipped hardest — and what's driving it?

Toronto's housing market has been one of the most volatile in North America. After a decade of upward pressure, the 2022 rate hikes inverted the dynamics almost overnight. Prices dropped. Monthly payments didn't.

Affordability is no longer a single number. Home prices, carrying costs, and local inventory each tell a different part of the story, and the headline figure on a listing site increasingly misses what buyers actually face. The goal of this analysis was to bring those three pressures into one view and ask: where does the real pain sit, and what's causing it?

02The Data

Four sources, stitched into one view.

  • TRREB MLS dataMonthly sale prices, listings, and inventory across Greater Toronto municipalities.
  • Bank of CanadaOvernight rate and posted 5-year fixed rate, used to derive monthly carrying cost.
  • Statistics CanadaCMA-level population and household income, for affordability ratios.
  • Timespan1998 through the most recent complete reporting year.
03Preparation

Shaping the raw inputs.

The bulk of the work was upstream. TRREB publishes its data in monthly files that change format periodically and use shifting municipality names — consolidating them into a single consistent long-form table was the first real lift.

From there: normalizing municipality names across years, correcting a handful of columns where sale averages had been reported alongside medians, and filling gaps where a municipality was renamed or re-defined mid-series.

Two calculated fields did most of the analytical work downstream: monthly carrying cost (price × mortgage rate / amortization schedule) and affordability ratio (carrying cost ÷ median household income). Re-expressing everything in those terms is what made regions comparable.

04Approach

Three pressures, compared side by side.

The analysis was framed around three pressures — price, supply, and cost of carry — and how they move together or apart.

Multi-year trend lines were built at the municipality level so regional divergence could be surfaced rather than washed out by a GTA-wide average. Each region was then indexed to its 2005 baseline, which made percentage change comparable across very different price tiers (a 10% shift in Durham doesn't mean the same thing as a 10% shift in central Toronto).

Finally, rate-change months were cross-referenced against price reactions to identify lag and sensitivity — which parts of the GTA respond fastest when the cost of money changes.

05Key Insights

What the data actually said.

Five findings, each with direct implications for buyers, policy, and investment decisions.

01

Prices fell. Monthly payments went up anyway.

Post-2022, the roughly 15% GTA price correction was fully offset by higher mortgage rates. Affordability, measured at the monthly payment level, did not improve — and in many municipalities, it got worse.

02

Supply constraints are regional, not uniform.

Peel and York saw the tightest inventory across the correction. Halton and Durham moved closer to balanced markets in the same period, creating very different local dynamics under the same headline narrative.

03

The flight to the 905 has partly reversed.

The suburban premium built up during 2020–2021 has been the first to soften. Outer municipalities are now cooling faster than the core — the opposite of the pandemic-era pattern.

04

Condos and detached homes have decoupled.

Condos held prices better than detached homes through the downturn, likely reflecting different buyer pools (entry-level vs. family formation) under different cost-of-carry pressure.

05

Affordability ratios have worsened everywhere — but the gap has narrowed.

Every sub-region is less affordable than it was in 2015. The surprising move is that core and outer municipalities have converged toward each other; the divide is less geographic than it used to be.

06Implications

What the insights mean in practice.

For buyers

Price alone isn't the right signal. Carrying cost is where the decision actually lives, and it's moving on different timelines than list price.

For investors

The regional divergence creates new entry points in Halton and Durham — but requires watching inventory, not just prices, to time them.

For policy

The "flight to 905" story needs updating. Outer markets are cooling faster than the core, which changes who current affordability programs should target.

08Reflection

What worked — and what I'd do differently next time.

What worked

The most impactful move wasn't new math — it was re-baselining everything to make regions comparable. Deriving carrying cost rather than leaning on list prices is what made the affordability story actually visible. A lot of housing narratives fall apart when you look at the monthly payment; this one held together because the framing accounted for it.

What I'd improve

I'd bring rent data in to complete the own-vs-rent affordability picture, which this dashboard currently leaves on the table. I'd also layer in forecasting on carrying cost under different rate scenarios — the single most common question a buyer asks ("what happens if rates go up one more point?") is the one the current view can't answer.
