Telecom Customer Churn Dashboard

Which customers are most at risk of leaving — and where would retention spend actually pay back? An exploration of churn behaviour across contract types, tenure, and service mix.

01The Question

Churn isn't just a revenue problem — it's an acquisition problem.

Every subscriber who leaves has to be replaced, and for most telecoms the cost of acquiring a new customer is several times the monthly margin of a retained one. That means the real cost of churn is usually disguised inside the sales line, not the revenue line.

The question this analysis sets out to answer is straightforward: which customer cohorts are most at risk of leaving, and what behaviours precede it? Once that's clear, retention offers can be pointed where they actually change outcomes — rather than sprayed at customers who were going to stay anyway.

02Approach

Segment the base. Let the risk concentrations speak.

The work starts from a standard telecom subscriber dataset — roughly 7,000 customers, 20 attributes, and a churn flag. The analytical move was to segment along four natural lines — tenure, contract length, payment method, and service mix — and look at how churn rate concentrates in each.

Cross-tabs across those dimensions surfaced where the risk actually lives. Where the data allowed, voluntary and involuntary churn were separated so pricing signals couldn't get confused with service-quality signals.

03Key Insights

Four findings that change where retention should focus.

01

Contract length is the single strongest predictor of churn.

Month-to-month customers churn at roughly 3× the rate of one-year contracts and around 7× the rate of two-year contracts. The contract term is more predictive than any single service or pricing attribute.

02

First-year customers are the most volatile.

Roughly half of all departures happen in the first twelve months. After month 12, churn rates drop sharply — meaning retention work has the highest leverage early in the relationship.

03

Fiber optic customers churn more than DSL — despite paying more.

Higher-tier subscribers leaving at higher rates is a service-quality signal, not a pricing signal. The usual retention discount won't fix this; it's telling you the product isn't delivering what the pricing promised.

04

Electronic-check payers churn almost twice as often as auto-pay customers.

The payment method itself isn't causing churn — it's a proxy for commitment. Customers who resist auto-pay are the same ones already half out the door. Moving them to auto-pay correlates with dramatically lower churn.

04Business Actions

Where the retention budget should go next quarter.

Target early-tenure risk

Incentivize contract upgrades in months 3–6, when first-year churn risk is concentrated. A small term extension here is worth more than a large discount later.

Investigate fiber service quality

The fiber churn signal is not a pricing problem. Before adjusting offers, operations should look at network and support quality — the usual retention lever won't hold here.

Convert e-check to auto-pay

A small incentive to switch payment method pays back twice: directly (payment friction drops) and indirectly (the switch itself correlates with much lower churn).

Stop discounting the already-loyal

Customers on 2-year contracts churn least. The biggest retention offers should not go to them — they should go to the cohorts where the dollar actually changes the outcome.
