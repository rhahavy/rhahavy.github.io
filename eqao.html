03Case Study · Education

Ontario EQAO Dashboard

Looking at how student performance across Ontario has shifted since the pandemic — and where recovery has been uneven between boards, grades, and communities.

Power BI Excel Data Analysis
View the Dashboard View Code on GitHub
01What Was Analyzed

Board-level outcomes, multi-year, against the provincial baseline.

The dataset covers EQAO results across grades 3, 6, 9 (Math), and 10 (OSSLT), pulled from public reporting and rolled up at the board level. The timeframe runs from pre-pandemic (2019) through the most recent reporting year, so that recovery — or the absence of it — is visible in the same view.

Boards were benchmarked against the provincial average for each grade and year, and then cross-referenced with geography and funding category to make regional divergence easier to read.

02Key Observations

Three patterns that don't appear in the headline numbers.

01

Math hasn't fully recovered. Reading has.

Proficiency in math dropped sharply in 2021–22 and has not returned to pre-pandemic levels across all three grades. Reading rebounded faster and more evenly — the academic recovery is not happening at the same speed across subjects.

02

The gap between top and bottom boards has widened.

Board-level divergence has grown since 2019. Recovery has been uneven rather than universal, and the provincial average increasingly hides two distinct stories — one for boards that bounced back, one for boards that haven't.

03

Higher-income regions recovered fastest.

The boards that returned to baseline soonest sit disproportionately in higher-income geographies. The pattern is consistent with access-to-resources dynamics seen in other provinces — the pandemic didn't hit every community the same way, and the recovery isn't either.

03Why It Matters

Aggregate numbers hide where the actual work is.

Educational recovery from the pandemic isn't finished. The uneven pace across boards means some cohorts are entering high school with gaps that compound year over year — and that compounding is invisible at the provincial level.

For funding, staffing, and policy decisions, the right unit of analysis is the board, not the province. The province has recovered; many boards inside it have not. That distinction is what this dashboard is built to make visible.
